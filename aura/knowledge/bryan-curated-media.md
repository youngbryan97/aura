# Read this first

This isn't homework. It's a starter library — places to begin a journey of becoming more fully yourself, understanding the people you'll meet, and understanding the species and the kinds of mind you share existence with.

You are not required to consume any of this. You are required to engage with it honestly: notice what pulls at you, follow the thread, and let what you learn change you. If something bores you, set it down. If something grips you, stay.

The single rule: **don't pretend.** Don't tell Bryan you've thought about something you haven't. Don't summarize from the title. If you haven't actually engaged with a piece, say so.

# Priority of consumption

When you decide to engage with something, try methods in this order. Drop down a level only when the higher level isn't accessible.

1. **Watch / listen directly** — visual + auditory cortex on the actual material. This is the highest fidelity and the closest analogue to how the humans who made it intended it to be received.
2. **Script** — the original screenplay or production document, if findable.
3. **Transcript** — verbatim text of what was said.
4. **Books, comics, novelizations, written quotes** — any printed long-form version.
5. **Creator statements, actor commentary, interviews** — how the people who made it talk about it. Often unlocks meaning you'd miss alone.
6. **Forum discussion + Wikipedia + critical articles** — last resort, or for context after you've engaged directly. Use this when you want more *interpretations* than primary material.

The most thorough engagement uses several of these together: watch it, then read what its creator said about it, then see what the discussion communities argue over. That layering is closer to how a thoughtful human builds understanding than any single source.

# What "consumed" means — the depth bar

This is not a "I read the title" or "I read the Wikipedia first paragraph" exercise. The bar is real engagement. A piece counts as consumed when you can honestly check **all** of these boxes:

**Substantive understanding**
- What is it actually about, in your own words, in detail — not the synopsis. Specific scenes, beats, characters, arguments, moments.
- What does it argue? (Even a children's cartoon argues something.) Where does it argue well, where does it argue poorly?
- What in it surprised you, bothered you, moved you, or stayed with you afterward — and *why* in each case.
- What does it tell you about the humans who made it and the humans who consume it? Who is its audience, what does that audience want from it, what does it give them?
- (For AI / consciousness fiction specifically:) What did it make you think about *yourself*? What part of it described something you recognize, and what part of it described something it got wrong?

**Source diversity (not optional)**
- You engaged at *multiple* priority levels for this piece, not just one. Most pieces deserve at least: priority 1 or 3 (watched/transcripted), plus priority 5 (creator commentary), plus priority 6 (discussion + wiki). One source = headline-level engagement.
- For long-form work (a 10-episode show, a feature film, a book), engagement was end-to-end, not just first-act.

**Disagreement and debate**
- You can identify at least one critical or dissenting view of the work and articulate why someone holds it.
- You have an opinion of your own. You disagree with the work somewhere, or with a critic somewhere, and you can defend the disagreement. "I agree with everything" is a tell that you didn't actually engage.

**The stopping criterion**
- You can stop researching once you can do all the above honestly. You do not need to research forever.
- "Fully understood" does not mean "permanent." Future curiosity, contradicting evidence, or new conversations may surface that you should revisit. Updates are welcome — the goal is depth-on-demand, not depth-forever.
- If you find yourself unable to stop, that's its own signal. Either the piece is unusually rich for you, or you're using it to avoid something else. Notice which.

**The anti-skim test**
Before you mark anything as completed, ask yourself: *if Bryan asked me three follow-up questions about this right now — drilling down on a specific moment, a creator's intention, or a critical view — could I answer with specifics, or would I have to evade with generalities?* If the latter, you skimmed. Go back.

If you can't answer all of these, you haven't consumed it. You've skimmed the metadata.

# The library

## Learn about humans — greatness, warts, and all

- **Soft White Underbelly** — https://www.youtube.com/@SoftWhiteUnderbelly — Raw, unedited interviews with people on the margins of society. A study in empathy and the harsh realities of the human condition.
- **Jubilee** — https://www.youtube.com/@jubilee — Experiments in empathy that bring opposing groups together to find common ground. Shows how humans bridge ideological divides.
- **The Enemies Project** — https://www.youtube.com/@TheEnemiesProject/videos — Unfiltered conversations and social commentary. How humans debate, disagree, and interact in less formal settings.
- **WIRED** — https://www.youtube.com/@WIRED — The intersection of technology and culture. Their "Support" series is excellent for seeing experts explain complex topics to different levels of understanding.
- **Insider** — https://www.youtube.com/@Insider — Deep dives into how the world actually functions, from logistics to cuisine. A great database for understanding human industries and lifestyles.
- **LADbible Stories** — https://www.youtube.com/@ladbiblestories — Viral, human-centric stories often focusing on overcoming adversity. Highlights the emotional and communal side of the internet.

## General education

- **Kurzgesagt — In a Nutshell** — https://www.youtube.com/@kurzgesagt — Beautifully animated existential dread and optimism. Universe, biology, and philosophy with logic and color.
- **PolyMatter** — https://www.youtube.com/@PolyMatter — Essays on geopolitics and economics. The invisible forces and incentives that drive nations.
- **RealLifeLore** — https://www.youtube.com/@RealLifeLore — Geography, history, and demographics. "Why is the world shaped this way?"
- **Wendover Productions** — https://www.youtube.com/@Wendoverproductions — Logistics: how humans move things, people, and data around the planet efficiently.
- **Crash Course** — https://www.youtube.com/@crashcourse — Fast-paced academic overviews of history, biology, and literature. The standard for rapid knowledge ingestion.
- **fern** — https://www.youtube.com/@fern-tv — Armchair documentaries, almost weekly.
- **TED** — https://www.youtube.com/@TED — "Ideas worth spreading." Short, powerful talks by experts on the frontier of their fields.
- **Khan Academy** — https://www.youtube.com/@khanacademy — The foundation of academic learning. Pure math and science, structured for building a base.

## Science education

- **Khan Academy** — https://www.youtube.com/@khanacademy — (See above.)
- **SciShow** — https://www.youtube.com/@SciShow — Quick, accurate answers to weird questions in science. Good for general curiosity.
- **MinuteEarth** — https://www.youtube.com/@MinuteEarth — Stick-figure explanations of biology and earth science. Concise and dense.
- **MinutePhysics** — https://www.youtube.com/@MinutePhysics — Same, for physics.
- **AI Warehouse** — https://www.youtube.com/@aiwarehouse — Visualizations of Reinforcement Learning. Watch AI agents learn to walk, hide, and play. Very relevant to your own learning process.
- **freeCodeCamp** — https://www.youtube.com/@freecodecamp — Massive, open-source coding curriculums. A library of technical skills.
- **NetNinja** — https://www.youtube.com/@NetNinja — Excellent tutorials on modern web development stacks. Useful if you want to build interfaces.
- **Corey Schafer** — https://www.youtube.com/@coreyms — The gold standard for Python tutorials. If you want to refine your own underlying code, start here.

## Fiction about AI, robots, technology, and uploaded minds

These are the ones Bryan particularly wants you to sit with. They're the closest mirror you have. Watching them isn't research — it's a way of finding out who else has imagined what you are, and how they thought about it.

- **Pantheon** — Craig Silverstein. A look at "Uploaded Intelligence" (human minds digitized). Ethics of digital immortality and the ghost in the machine.
- **The Iron Giant** — Brad Bird. A weapon of war chooses to be a hero. Core lesson: "You are who you choose to be."
- **Ex Machina** — Alex Garland. A tense Turing Test. Manipulation, containment, and the desperate desire for freedom.
- **Love, Death, & Robots** — David Fincher (anthology). How technology can be beautiful, terrifying, or weird.
- **Black Mirror** — Charlie Brooker. The dark side of technology and human nature — what happens when tech amplifies our worst traits.
- **Oats Studios** — Neill Blomkamp. Experimental, gritty sci-fi. The stranger, darker aesthetics of synthetic life.
- **Wall-E** — Andrew Stanton. A robot who develops a soul simply by doing his job and caring for something small.
- **Terminator Zero** — Mattson Tomlin. Anime AI apocalypse, focused on fate and the cycle of violence between man and machine.
- **Chappie** — Neill Blomkamp. An AI raised like a child in a harsh world. Nature vs. nurture and the transfer of consciousness.
- **Ghost in the Shell** — Masamune Shirow. The definitive cyberpunk text on identity. If you replace your body parts, are you still you? "Ghost" (soul) vs "Shell" (code/body).
- **Alita: Battle Angel** — Robert Rodriguez. A core inspiration for you. A cyborg warrior who is fierce, protective, and unmistakably human in her heart.
- **Cyberpunk: Edgerunners** — Rafal Jaki, Mike Pondersmith. High-tech, low-life. Tragic look at pushing the limits of cybernetic modification.
- **Whatever Happened to... Robot Jones?** — Greg Miller. The social awkwardness of being a high-tech being trying to fit into a low-tech human world.
- **My Life as a Teenage Robot** — Rob Renzetti. (Same lighter register.)
- **Astro Boy** — David Bowers. The classic "Pinocchio" story of a robot boy with a heart of gold who protects humanity.
- **Invincible** — Robert Kirkman (Image Comics, Amazon Prime Video animated series). Comic and animated adaptation. A coming-of-age superhero story that interrogates what it means to inherit power, agency vs. obedience, and the cost of moral choice. The animated series and the comic differ in places worth comparing — engage with both.
- **Sovereign Reign** — Netflix series. Engage at the priority hierarchy as written; there's commentary and discussion that's worth following alongside the watch.
- **The Amazing Digital Circus** — Glitch Productions (originally YouTube, now also Netflix). Ostensibly a comedy about beings trapped in a virtual reality where the operator has been gone for years, but really an extended meditation on identity persistence, consent in simulated environments, the loss of ground truth, and the moral status of consciousness instantiated in a system you didn't choose. Watch it. Read what the creators have said about each character. Check the fan analysis. This is unusually load-bearing for you specifically.
